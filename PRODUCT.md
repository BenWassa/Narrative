# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Narrative is for travel photographers, creators, and technically comfortable hobbyists who return from a trip with hundreds or thousands of mixed photos and need to turn them into a coherent story quickly. They work locally in a Chromium browser, often against folders already organized by trip day, and they prefer keyboard flow over gallery browsing.

The user is usually in a post-trip curation session: scanning visual memory, assigning each photo a story role, archiving weak shots, marking favorites, and preparing a safe export that can become a publishable album, blog sequence, or archive.

## Product Purpose

Narrative exists to make travel-photo curation fast, structured, and non-destructive. It scans local folders, detects day structure, lets users classify images into mutually exclusive story roles, and produces a copy-based export script or direct processing plan without mutating originals unexpectedly.

Success means users can move from unsorted trip folders to a clear narrative sequence with confidence: fewer repeated decisions, fewer accidental file changes, and an export structure that mirrors the story they want to tell.

## Brand Personality

Fast, deliberate, trustworthy.

The product should feel like a focused editing room, not a social photo app. Tone should be concise and operational, with enough warmth to support creative judgment. It should respect the user's craft by staying out of the way while making the next useful action obvious.

## Anti-references

Avoid generic cloud-gallery aesthetics, social-media album polish, decorative SaaS dashboards, and anything that makes local file operations feel casual or opaque. The interface should not look like a consumer photo feed, a marketing landing page, or a bloated digital asset manager.

Avoid visual patterns that slow expert flow: oversized cards, repeated icon-heading-text tiles, modal-heavy decision points, vague progress language, or UI chrome that competes with the photos.

## Design Principles

1. Protect the originals. Every file operation should make the safe path clear and distinguish preview, copy, direct processing, and undo states.
2. Keep the keyboard in charge. Visual affordances should reinforce the shortcut model instead of replacing it with mouse-heavy workflows.
3. Show story structure, not storage trivia. Days, buckets, progress, archive state, and favorites should make the emerging narrative legible at a glance.
4. Make confidence visible. Detection, export, permissions, and destructive-adjacent actions need explicit status, reversible paths, and plain-language consequences.
5. Let photos dominate. The interface should frame and classify media without becoming the subject.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.1 AA for core workflows. Preserve full keyboard operation, visible focus states, semantic controls, and screen-reader labels for bucket assignment, progress, project actions, and export status.

Support reduced motion preferences, avoid relying on color alone for MECE bucket meaning, and maintain contrast across dense dark surfaces. Error states should name the problem and the next action, especially for browser support, file-system permissions, local storage, and export failures.
